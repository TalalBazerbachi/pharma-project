Redoxon contains the active ingredient pure vitamin C
(ascorbic acid). Vitamin C is involved in a variety of vital
metabolic processes through various mechanisms.
It is used for the treatment and prophylaxis of vitamin C
deficiency syndromes such as scurvy and its precursor
stages.
For the following diseases, vitamin C requirements may be
increased and/or administration of supplementary vitamin
C may be required:
- Bleeding gums associated with vitamin C deficiency;
- Wound healing, for example after tooth extraction;
- Operations;
- Infectious diseases;
- Colds;
- Absorption disorders after operational removal of
the stomach (gastrectomy) or after gastrointestinal
diseases;
- Smoking;
- Pregnancy and breastfeeding;
- Treatment with antibiotics;
- Haemodialysis.
Other factors that need to be considered
As the body cannot produce vitamin C itself, it must be
ingested along with food. Vegetables and fruits, especially
citrus fruits such as oranges, lemons and grapefruit, are
rich in vitamin C. Large amounts of vitamin C can also be

found in liver and kidneys, but not in meat, cereals and
dairy products.
Cooking, rinsing, keeping warm and storing (including
freezing) can heavily reduce vitamin C content. Diabetics
who measure their urinary glucose levels themselves
should not take Redoxon for several days before the test,
as vitamin C can affect the result.


If you are allergic to one or more of the ingredients.
You should not take Redoxon if you suffer from kidney
stones with concurrent increased oxalic acid excretion,
have an iron storage disease or if you suffer from severe
renal impairment or renal failure (GFR < 30 mg/min)
including dialysis patients.
If you experience an allergic reaction during treatment
with Redoxon, you should stop taking the product.
Warnings and precautions
Patients with kidney stones should only take more than
500 mg Redoxon daily after consulting their doctor.
Redoxon is sweetened with aspartame. Patients with
phenylketonuria should therefore consult their doctor
before taking the product.
If you suffer from an intolerance to fructose (very rare),
you should not take Redoxon as it contains sorbitol (the
metabolic breakdown of sorbitol produces fructose).
Contraceptive drugs (oral contraceptives) and certain
medications used to treat allergies and inflammation
(corticosteroids) lead to increased breakdown of vitamin
C. Certain medications that inhibit bone resorption
(calcitonin) increase consumption of vitamin C. Derivatives
of salicylic acid (e.g. acetylsalicylic acid), certain antibiotics
(tetracyclines) and certain drugs used to treat epilepsy
(barbiturates) reduce the availability of vitamin C in the
body through reduced absorption or increased excretion.
High doses of vitamin C can lower the concentrations of
ciclosporin, indinavir, warfarin and disulfiram in the blood.
Concurrent administration of vitamin C and products
containing aluminium for heartburn (antacids) can lead
to increased uptake of aluminium. Caution is especially
advised if you suffer from kidney failure.
High doses of vitamin C can increase the level of
ethinyl estradiol (contraceptive pill) in the blood. When
discontinuing vitamin C, there is a risk of spotting and
failure of the contraceptive effect.
Please inform your doctor, pharmacist or druggist if you
are suffering from other diseases, have an allergy or are

taking other medications (including those you purchase
yourself).
Pregnancy and breastfeeding
There are no concerns for taking Redoxon during pregnancy
or when breastfeeding.


Unless otherwise prescribed by your doctor:
Adults and adolescents over 12 years:
- Take 500mg up to 1 g of Redoxon daily for vitamin
C deficiency and gastrointestinal diseases with
absorption disorders (impaired uptake into the blood).
500 mg daily is usually sufficient to meet increased
requirements.
- Take 1 g daily for colds.
Effervescent tablets: Add 1 effervescent tablet to approx.
150 ml of water. Redoxon is ready to drink as soon as it has
fully dissolved.
Redoxon is not suitable for use in children under 12 years.
Always take the dosage stated in this package leaflet or
prescribed by your doctor. If you think that the effect of
the medicine is too weak or too strong, please consult your
doctor, pharmacist or druggist.


following side effects may occur when taking Redoxon:
Administration may lead to allergic reactions (e.g.
rash, hives, oedema, itching), asthma, shortness of
breath, pulmonary spasms, gastrointestinal disorders
(e.g. diarrhoea, nausea, vomiting, abdominal and

Occasional mild diarrhoea or increased urination and

If you notice any side effects not listed in this leaflet, please

Redoxon effervescent tablet

Effervescent tablets (orange flavoured) Active substance: Ascorbic acid. Excipients: Flavourings; aspartame; acesulfame K; excipients for tablets.

Pharmaceutical form and quantity of active substance per unit One effervescent tablet contains:1 g ascorbic acid (vitamin C) per tablet, gentle on the teeth (25 kJ = 6 kcal).

Prevention and treatment of ascorbic acid deficiency syndromes such as scurvy, pre-scurvy and Moeller-Barlow disease.
In the following circumstances, ascorbic acid requirements may be increased and/or administration of supplementary ascorbic acid
may be required:
– Gum bleeding due to ascorbic acid deficiency
– Wound healing, e.g. after tooth extractions
– Surgery
– Infectious diseases
– Colds
– Malabsorption (gastroenteropathy), gastrectomy
– Smoking
– Pregnancy/lactation
– Antibiotic therapy

– Haemodialysis
– Methaemoglobinaemia.


Usual dosage
Adults and adolescents from 12 years:
In ascorbic acid deficiency states and in gastrointestinal disorders associated with malabsorption: 500 mg daily (one chewable tablet)
to 1 g (two chewable tablets; one effervescent tablet) orally.
500 mg daily is usually sufficient to meet increased requirements.
Colds: 1 g per day.
Special populations
In the case of enzymatic (familial idiopathic) methaemoglobinaemia, treatment may be switched to oral administration of ascorbic acid
after an initial intravenous injection.

Redoxon is not suitable for use in children under 12 years.


Hypersensitivity to one of the ingredients listed under “Composition”. Nephrolithiasis and oxaluria associated with acidic and normal urinary pH. Severe renal impairment or renal failure (GFR < 30 mg/min) including dialysis patients. Iron storage diseases, e.g. haemochromatosis.

Because ascorbic acid can increase iron absorption, caution is advised in patients with iron storage disease, haemochromatosis or
disorders such as beta thalassaemia.
Acute or chronic overdose of ascorbic acid can lead to renal damage (see “Overdose” section).
Before taking Redoxon, the concurrent use of other (multi)vitamin products or the presence of renal impairment (see
"Contraindications" and “Overdose" sections) must be considered.
An overdose of ascorbic acid in individuals with glucose-6-phosphate dehydrogenase deficiency is associated with haemolytic
anaemia (see “Overdose” section).
Patients with phenylketonuria should be informed that Redoxon contains aspartame.

Patients with fructose intolerance should be informed that Redoxon chewable tablets contain sorbitol (the metabolic breakdown of
which produces fructose).


Interactions Corticosteroids increase oxidation.
Calcitonin causes increased consumption of ascorbic acid.
Salicylates inhibit the active transport of ascorbic acid across the intestinal wall and increase its renal elimination.
Tetracyclines inhibit intracellular metabolism and tubular reabsorption, thus increasing the tubular elimination of ascorbic acid in the
urine.
Antacids: Ascorbic acid may increase the systemic uptake of aluminium in aluminium-containing antacids. This is particularly
important to consider in patients with renal impairment.
Iron: Ascorbic acid increases iron absorption, especially in individuals with iron deficiency. This can lead to iron overload in individuals
with haemochromatosis or hereditary carriers of the disorder (see “Contraindications” section).
Ascorbic acid increases the toxic effects of iron, especially on the heart, and can lead to cardiac decompensation.
Cyclosporin, indinavir, warfarin and disulfiram : high doses of ascorbic acid can reduce serum concentrations of these substances.
Barbiturates increase the elimination of ascorbic acid in the urine.


There is no evidence of a risk during pregnancy. Redoxon may therefore also be used during pregnancy and lactation.


No relevant studies have been carried out. In all likelihood Redoxon has no influence on reactions.


The adverse effects listed are based on spontaneous reports. It is therefore not appropriate to arrange them
by CIOMS III frequency category.
Immune system disorders
Allergic and anaphylactic reactions, including anaphylactic shock.
Hypersensitivity reactions include asthma syndrome, mild to moderate reactions of the skin (rash, urticaria,
oedema, pruritus), the airways, the gastrointestinal tract and the cardiovascular system. Potential symptoms
are rash, urticaria, pruritus, allergic rash, allergic oedema, diarrhoea, abdominal pain, dyspnoea, bronchial
spasms and hypotension.
Gastrointestinal disorders:
Diarrhoea, nausea, vomiting, abdominal and gastrointestinal pain.


Single doses of more than 4–5 g occasionally – and those of 10 g and more almost always – cause transient
osmotic diarrhoea accompanied by abdominal symptoms.

Treatment: Reduce ascorbic acid intake and provide symptomatic treatment.
Chronic overdose (> 4 g ascorbic acid daily) can lead to the formation of renal stones and in isolated cases
to haemolysis.


ATC code: A11GA01
Together with the metabolite dehydroascorbic acid, which is also biologically active, ascorbic acid forms a
reversible redox system which is involved in many enzymatic reactions and forms the basis for the spectrum
of activity of ascorbic acid. The importance of ascorbic acid in humans is most clearly evident in clinically
manifest ascorbic acid deficiency, i.e. scurvy. Ascorbic acid plays a key role in the production of
hydroxyproline from proline, which, in turn, is essential for the development of functionally active collagen.
The symptoms of scurvy, e.g. slow wound healing, bone growth disorders, increased vascular fragility and
impaired dentine synthesis, are consequences of impaired collagen synthesis.
The presence of sufficient ascorbic acid is required for the biosynthesis of carnitine from certain proteins
carrying terminal lysine and methionine residues; consequently, carnitine deficiency in the muscles may be
an early sign of ascorbic acid deficiency.
The conversion of epinephrine to norepinephrine requires ascorbic acid. Furthermore, ascorbic acid protects
these catecholamines from being oxidised to neurotoxic adrenochromes in neural tissue.

Ascorbic acid promotes cortisol synthesis, so that reduced amounts of glucocorticoids are released in
patients with ascorbic acid deficiency.
One key function of ascorbic acid is the inactivation of free radicals, which can destroy cell membranes by
lipid peroxidation. This function is particularly well known in the eye, where ascorbic acid prevents the
photochemical formation of oxygen radicals, which can damage the retina.
Elevated histamine levels have been found in the plasma of patients with ascorbic acid deficiency. It is
thought that ascorbic acid interferes with the breakdown and excretion of histamine. Ascorbic acid protects
against iron deficiency anaemia by improving iron absorption from food.
Ascorbic acid supports the immune system.


No pharmacokinetic studies have been carried out with this product. The following information appears in
the literature:
Absorption
Ascorbic acid is primarily absorbed in the upper part of the small intestine via sodium-dependent active
transport. At higher concentrations, absorption takes place via passive diffusion. 70–90% of the substance is
absorbed after an oral dose of up to approximately 180 mg. If 1–12 g is taken, the absorption rate falls from
around 50% to around 15%, although the absolute quantity absorbed continues to increase.

Distribution
Approximately 24% of ascorbic acid is bound to plasma proteins. Serum concentrations are normally around
10 mg/L (60 μmol/L). Concentrations below 6 mg/L (35 μmol/L) indicate that intake is not always sufficient,
while those below 4 mg/L (20 μmol/L) indicate that it is insufficient. Clinically manifest scurvy occurs at
serum concentrations below 2 mg/L (10 μmol/L).
Metabolism
Ascorbic acid is partly metabolised via dehydroascorbic acid to oxalic acid. If excessive amounts are
ingested, however, most of the ascorbic acid is excreted in unchanged form in the urine and faeces.
Ascorbic acid-2-sulphate also appears in the urine as a metabolite.
Elimination
The physiological body pool is around 1,500 mg. The elimination half-life of ascorbic acid depends on the
route of administration, the quantity ingested and the absorption rate. After ingestion of around 50 mg
ascorbic acid, the half-life is around 14 days; after ingestion of 1 g it is around 13 hours. If less than 1–3 g of
ascorbic acid per day is taken, it is excreted primarily via the kidneys. In the case of doses in excess of 3 g,
increasing amounts are excreted in unchanged form in the faeces.

The effervescent tablets and chewable tablets should be stored in a dry place at temperature not above 25°C.


Effervescent tablets: packs of 10 & 15 tablets


Influence on diagnostic methods
In diabetics, ascorbic acid may interfere with detection of glucose in the urine but does not affect
hyperglycaemia. Ascorbic acid use should therefore be discontinued several days before testing for urinary
glucose.
Ascorbic acid can cause false negative results in tests for the presence of blood in stool samples.
