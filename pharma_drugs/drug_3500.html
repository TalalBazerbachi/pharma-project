BI-TILDIEM 90 mg, prolonged-release coated tablet

Diltiazem hydrochloride ........................................................................................... 90 mg for one prolonged-release coated tablet. For the full list of excipient see section 6.1.

Prolonged-release coated tablet

Prevention of attacks of stable angina.


Treatment should be initiated with 1 x 90 mg tablet in the morning and in the evening. If necessary, dosage can be increased to 1 x 120 mg tablet, and, in exceptional cases, to 2 x 90 mg tablets, in the morning and in the evening.
In elderly subjects, especially those who have bradycardia or who are given multiple drug treatments, and in patients with kidney or liver failure, the daily dosage must not exceed 1 x 90 mg tablet in the morning and in the evening (see section 4.4) except in very specific cases in which it may be increased.
In children, since the safety and efficacy of the drug have not been established, use of diltiazem is not recommended.
Diltiazem should be used with caution in patients with kidney or liver failure (see section 4.4).
Tablets should not be chewed but swallowed with a little water.
Once the active substance has been released, the empty tablet matrix is usually found in the feces.

Many antiarrythmics have depressant effects on cardiac automaticity, conduction and contractility.
Combined use of antiarrhythmics of different classes can have a beneficial therapeutic effect, but is
most often problematic, and requires close clinical monitoring and ECG monitoring. Combined use
of antiarrythmics that induce torsades de pointes (amiodarone, disopyramide, quinidines, sotalol, etc.)
is contraindicated.
Combined use of antiarrhythmics of the same class is not recommended, except in exceptional cases,
due to the higher risk of undesirable cardiac effects.
Combination with drugs that have negative inotropic properties, bradycardia-inducing effects and/or
medicines that slow atrioventricular conduction can be problematic and requires clinical and ECG
monitoring (amiodarone, bepridil, cibenzoline, diltiazem, disopyramide, dofetilide, flecainide,
hydroquinidine, ibutilide, lidocaine, mexiletine, propafenone, quinidine, sotalol, verapamil).
Drugs with bradycardia-inducing effects
Many drugs can induce bradycardia, particularly class Ia antiarrythmics, beta-blockers, certain class
III antiarrhythmics, certain calcium channel blockers, digitalis glycosides, pilocarpine,
anticholinesterase agents, etc.
Contraindicated combinations
+ Cisapride
Increased risk of ventricular arrhythmias, particularly torsades de pointes.
+ Dantrolene infusion
Cases of fatal ventricular fibrillation are regularly observed in animals following IV administration of
verapamil and dantrolene. Therefore, combination of a calcium channel blocker with dantrolene is
potentially dangerous. However, some patients have received concomitant treatment with nifedipine
and dantrolene without any problems.
+ Dihydroergotamine
Ergotism with possible necrosis of the extremities (inhibited hepatic metabolism of rye ergot
alkaloids).
+ Ergotamine
Ergotism with possible necrosis of the extremities (inhibited hepatic metabolism of rye ergot
alkaloids).
+ Nifedipine
Significant increase in nifedipine concentrations due to reduction of its hepatic metabolism by
diltiazem, with risk of severe hypotension.
+ Pimozide
Increased risk of ventricular arrhythmias, particularly torsades de pointes.
+ Sertindole
Increased risk of ventricular arrhythmias, particularly torsades de pointes.
Inadvisable combinations
+ Beta-blockers (excluding esmolol)
Automaticity disorders (excessive bradycardia, sinus arrest), sinoatrial and atrioventricular
conduction disorders, and heart failure.
This combination requires close clinical and ECG monitoring, particularly in elderly subjects and at
the start of treatment.
+ Beta-blockers used in the treatment of heart failure (bisoprolol, carvedilol, metoprolol,
nebivolol)
Negative inotropic effect with a risk of decompensation of heart failure, automaticity disorders
(bradycardia, sinus arrest) and sinoatrial and atrioventricular conduction disorders.
+ Esmolol, in patients with impaired left ventricular function
Automaticity disorders (excessive bradycardia, sinus arrest), sinoatrial and atrioventricular
conduction disorders and heart failure.
+ Ivabradine
Increased ivabradine plasma concentrations and undesirable effects, particularly those affecting the
heart (diltiazem inhibits the hepatic metabolism of ivabradine), adding to the bradycardia-inducing
effects of these drugs.
+ Triazolam
Increased triazolam plasma concentrations due to reduced hepatic metabolism, with increased
sedation.
Combinations requiring precautions for use
+ Alfentanil
Increased respiratory depressant effect of the opiate analgesic due to reduced hepatic metabolism.
The dose of the analgesic should be adjusted if used with diltiazem.
+ Amiodarone
Risk of bradycardia and atrioventricular block, particularly in elderly subjects.
Clinical and ECG monitoring.
+ Enzyme-inducing antiepileptic drugs
Reduced plasma concentrations of the calcium channel blocker due to increased hepatic metabolism
by the inducer.
Clinical monitoring is required, and, if necessary, calcium channel blocker dose adjustment during
and after treatment with the inducer.
+ Atorvastatin
Increased risk of undesirable effects (concentration-dependent) such as rhabdomyolysis due to
reduced hepatic metabolism of the cholesterol-lowering drug. Lower doses of the cholesterollowering
drug or another statin not affected by this type of interaction should be used.
+ Baclofen
Increased risk of hypotension, particularly postural hypotension.
Monitoring of blood pressure and antihypertensive dose adjustment if necessary.
+ Buspirone
Increased buspirone plasma concentrations due to reduction of its hepatic metabolism by diltiazem,
with an increase in undesirable effects.
Clinical monitoring and buspirone dose adjustment if necessary.
+ Dronedarone
Risk of bradycardia and atrioventricular block, particularly in elderly subjects. Furthermore, a slight
increase in dronedarone concentrations due to a reduction of its metabolism by the calcium channel
blocker.
Calcium channel blocker treatment should be initiated at the minimum recommended dosage and the
doses adjusted on the basis of the ECG.
+ Esmolol, in patients with normal left ventricular function
Automaticity disorders (excessive bradycardia, sinus arrest), sinoatrial and atrioventricular
conduction disorders and heart failure.
Clinical and ECG monitoring.
+ Immunosuppressants (ciclosporin, everolimus, sirolimus, tacrolimus)
Increased blood concentrations of the immunosuppressant due to reduced metabolism.
Assay of the blood concentrations of the immunosuppressant, monitoring of kidney function and
dose adjustment during and after treatment.
+ Midazolam
Increased plasma midazolam concentrations due to reduced hepatic metabolism, with increased
sedation.
Clinical monitoring and reduction of dosage during treatment with diltiazem.
+ Medicinal products likely to induce torsades de pointes (excluding pimozide, cisapride): class
Ia antiarrhythmics (quinidine, hydroquinidine, disopyramide); class III antiarrhythmics
(amiodarone, dofetilide, ibutilide, sotalol); certain neuroleptics: phenothiazines
(chlorpromazine, cyamemazine, fluphenazine, levomepromazine), benzamides (amisulpride,
sulpiride, sultopride, tiapride), butyrophenones (droperidol, haloperidol), pimozide,
pipamperone, pipotiazine, zuclopenthixol; other: bepridil, diphemanil, erythromycin IV,
halofantrine, lumefantrine, methadone, mizolastine, moxifloxacin, pentamidine, spiramycin IV,
vincamine IV
Increased risk of ventricular arrhythmias, particularly torsades de pointes.
Clinical and ECG monitoring.
+ Rifampicin
Reduced plasma concentrations of the calcium channel blocker due to increased hepatic metabolism.
Clinical monitoring and, if necessary, calcium channel blocker dose adjustment during and after
treatment with rifampicin.
+ Simvastatin
Increased risk of undesirable effects (dose-dependent) such as rhabdomyolysis due to reduced hepatic
metabolism of the cholesterol-lowering drug. The dosage of 20 mg/day of simvastatin should not be
exceeded, or another statin not affected by this type of interaction should be administered.
Combinations to be taken into account
+ Alpha-blockers for urologic use (alfuzosin, doxazosin, prazosin, tamsulosin, terazosin)
Increased hypotensive effect. Increased risk of postural hypotension.
+ Amifostine
Increased risk of hypotension, particularly postural.
+ Imipramine antidepressants
Increased risk of hypotension, particularly postural.
+ Alpha-blocking antihypertensives
Increased hypotensive effect. Increased risk of postural hypotension.
+ Other bradycardia-inducing drugs
Risk of excessive bradycardia (additive effects).
+ Clonidine, guanfacine
Automaticity disorders (atrioventricular conduction disorders due to additive undesirable effects on
conduction).
+ Glucocorticoids (excluding hydrocortisone used as replacement therapy) and
mineralocorticoids
Reduced antihypertensive effect (corticosteroid water and sodium retention).
+ Neuroleptics
Increased risk of hypotension, particularly postural.
+ Pilocarpine
Risk of excessive bradycardia (additive bradycardic effects).
+ Nitrates, nitrites and related drugs
Increased risk of hypotension, particularly postural.


Pregnancy
Animal studies (rat, mouse, rabbit) have demonstrated teratogenic effects.
There are currently not enough data to evaluate the possible malformative and fetotoxic effect of
diltiazem hydrochloride when administered during pregnancy. Therefore, use of diltiazem
hydrochloride is not recommended during pregnancy or in women of child-bearing potential not
using contraceptives.
Lactation
Diltiazem is found at very low concentrations in breast milk.
However, breast-feeding should be avoided during treatment with diltiazem. If the use of diltiazem is
necessary, the infant should be fed using an alternative method.


On the basis of the adverse effects reported, such as dizziness (common), malaise (common), ability

Classification of undesirable effects according to expected frequencies: very common (≥ 1/10);
common (≥ 1/100, < 1/10); uncommon (≥ 1/1,000, < 1/100); rare (≥ 1/10,000, < 1/1,000); very rare
(< 1/10,000); not known (cannot be estimated from the available data).
Within each group frequency, undesirable effects are presented in order of decreasing seriousness.
Cardiac disorders
Common: atrioventricular block (which may be first, second or third degree; possible bundle-branch
block), palpitations.
Uncommon: bradycardia.
Not known: sinoatrial block and congestive heart failure.
Vascular disorders
Effects caused by vasodilation (headache, flushing, and in particular, edema of the lower limbs) are
dose-dependent and are related to the pharmacological activity of the active substance. These occur
more readily in elderly subjects.
Common: flushing.
Uncommon: postural hypotension.
Not known: vasculitis (including leukocytoclastic vasculitis).
Gastrointestinal disorders
Common: constipation, dyspepsia, epigastric pain, nausea.
Uncommon: vomiting, diarrhea.
Rare: dry mouth.
Not known: gingival hyperplasia.
Skin and subcutaneous tissue disorders
Common: erythema.
Rare: urticaria.
Not known: angioedema, rash, erythema multiforme (particularly Stevens-Johnson syndrome and
toxic epidermic necrolysis), exfoliative dermatitis, acute generalized exanthematous pustulosis,
photosensitivity reaction (in particular lichenoid keratosis on areas of skin exposed to the sun),
sweating, erythema, sometimes with fever and/or desquamation.
Hepatobiliary disorders
Uncommon: isolated, moderate, and generally transient increases in hepatic enzymes (AST, ALT,
LDH, alkaline phosphatase) have been observed during the initial treatment period.
Not known: clinical hepatitis reversible on treatment discontinuation.
Nervous system disorders
Common: headache, dizziness.
Not known: extrapyramidal effects, generally reversible on treatment discontinuation.
Psychiatric disorders
Uncommon: nervousness, insomnia.
Not known: mood changes (especially depression).
Reproductive system disorders
Not known: gynecomastia generally reversible on treatment discontinuation.
General disorders and administration site conditions
Very common: swelling of the lower limbs.
Common: malaise, asthenia.
Blood and lymphatic system disorders
Not known: thrombocytopenia.


The clinical picture for massive acute poisoning can include marked hypotension that can progress to
collapse, sinus bradycardia with or without isorhythmic dissociation and atrioventricular conduction
disorders.
The measures to be taken in a hospital environment include: gastric lavage, osmotic diuresis.
Conduction disorders may benefit from temporary electrosystolic pacing.
The antidotes proposed are: atropine, adrenaline, vasopressor substances, positive inotropic and
chronotropic agents, glucagon and calcium gluconate by infusion.


SELECTIVE CALCIUM ANTAGONIST WITH DIRECT CARDIAC EFFECTS /
BENZOTHIAZEPINE DERIVATIVES
(C08DB01: cardiovascular system)
Diltiazem slows calcium entry through membranes in vascular myocardial muscle fibre and smooth
muscle fibre and thus decreases the quantity of intracellular calcium reaching contractile proteins.
In animals
Diltiazem increases coronary flow without causing the phenomenon of coronary steal. It acts on the
small coronary arteries, on the large arterial trunks, on the collateral arteries.
This vasodilator effect, which is exerted moderately on peripheral systemic arterial regions, is
observed at doses which are not negatively inotropic.
The two main active circulating metabolites (desacetyl diltiazem and N-monodemethyl diltiazem)
have a pharmacological activity, in angina pectoris, of approximately 10 and 20% of that of diltiazem
hydrochloride.
In humans
Diltiazem increases coronary flow by reducing resistance.
Due to its moderate bradycardiac action and its moderate reduction in systemic arterial resistance,
diltiazem reduces the cardiac workload.
No negative inotropic effect has been demonstrated on a healthy myocardium. Diltiazem induces a
moderate slowing in heart rate and can have a depressant effect on a diseased sinus node. It slows
down atrioventricular conduction, with a risk of atrioventricular block. Diltiazem has no effect on
hisian and infra-hisian conduction.


The bioavailability of diltiazem in prolonged-release tablet form is around 40%, due to the liver firstpass
effect. Diltiazem is bound to plasma proteins in a proportion of 80-85%.
The peak plasma concentration is reached in around 4 to 8 hours after intake of the prolonged-release
tablet. Diltiazem is strongly metabolised by the liver; the main circulating metabolite is N-demethyl
diltiazem. The apparent plasma half-life is, on average, 7 to 8 hours. Only 0.2 to 4% unchanged
diltiazem is found in the urine.
Following repeated administration, there is a 30% increase in the following parameters: Cmax, AUC,
Cmin, relative to the values obtained following single-dose administration. But within the same
patient, the plasma concentrations are stable.
Plasma concentrations in the elderly and in subjects with impaired liver function are, on average,
higher, at equal doses, due to an increase in bioavailability. In patients with impaired kidney function,
a dosage reduction is only necessary on the basis of clinical response.
Diltiazem and its metabolites are barely dialysable.
