Pharmacotherapeutic group
SELECTIVE CALCIUM CHANNEL BLOCKERS WITH DIRECT CARDIAC EFFECTS/
BENZOTHIAZEPINE DERIVATIVES
This medicine belongs to the class of calcium channel blockers.
Therapeutic indications
This medicine is used to prevent angina attacks (symptoms related to failure of the
blood vessels that supply blood to the heart), particularly in patients with effort
angina or unstable angina including variant angina.


Contraindications
If you have been told by your doctor that you are intolerant to some sugars, contact
him/her before taking this medicine.
Do not take Tildiem 60 mg tablets in the following situations:
• allergy to diltiazem or to any of the other ingredients
• very slow heart rate (less than or equal to 40 beats per minute)
• sinus node dysfunction (heart rate disorder)
• second- or third-degree atrioventricular block not corrected by a pacemaker (heart
conductiondisorders)
• left ventricular failure with congestion in the lungs (impaired heart function)
• incombinationwithdantrolene(byinfusion),pimozide,cisapride,dihydroergotamine,
ergotamine, nifedipine, sertindole.
Unless otherwise indicated by your doctor, this medicine should generally not be used during pregnancy or in combination with esmolol (in certain cases), beta- blockers used for the treatment of heart failure (bisoprolol, carvedilol, metoprolol, nebivolol), other beta-blockers, ivabradine or triazolam.
Precautions for use; special warnings
Take special care with Tildiem 60 mg tablets
Special warnings
This medicine contains castor oil which can cause digestive disorders (mild laxative
effect, diarrhea).
This medicine should not be used in patients with galactose intolerance, Lapp lactase
deficiency or glucose/galactose malabsorption syndrome (rare hereditary diseases).
ALWAYS KEEP OUT OF THE REACH OF CHILDREN.
Precautions for use
This medicine should be used WITH CAUTION:
• in elderly subjects,
• in patients with kidney or liver failure,
• in patients with low heart rate or certain heart conduction disorders.
If you are to undergo general anesthesia, tell your doctor that you are taking diltiazem. This medicine can be associated with mood disorders (for example, depression). This medicine acts on intestinal motility. It must be used with caution in patients at risk of developing an intestinal obstruction.
Follow your doctor’s advice.
Other medicines and Tildiem 60 mg tablets
TO AVOID POSSIBLE INTERACTIONS BETWEEN SEVERAL MEDICINES, particularly

if you are taking dantrolene (infusion), pimozide, cisapride, dihydroergotamine, ergotamine, nifedipine, sertindole, esmolol (in certain cases), beta-blockers used in the treatment of heart failure (bisoprolol, carvedilol, metoprolol, nebivolol), other beta-blockers, ivabridine or triazolam.
ALWAYS INFORM YOUR DOCTOR OR PHARMACIST OF ANY OTHER TREATMENT YOU MAY BE TAKING.
Pregnancy and breast-feeding Pregnancy
Use of this medicine is not recommended during pregnancy or in women of child- bearing age who are not using contraception. If you discover that you are pregnant, tell your doctor immediately and follow his/her instructions with regard to your treatment. Tell your doctor if you are planning to have a baby.
Breast-feeding
This medicince should be avoided if you are breast-feeding. If the use of Tildiem 60 mg tablets is necessary, the infant should be fed using an alternative method (bottles, etc.). Ask your doctor or pharmacist for advice before taking any medicine.
Driving and using machines
The ability to drive or use machines may be impaired.
List of excipients with specific effects: hydrogenated castor oil, lactose.


How many Tildiem 60 mg tablets you should take
Adults: The usual dosage is 1 tablet 3 times daily.
Dosage depends on the patient’s condition and medical prescription.
Children: As the safety and efficacy of this medicine have not been demonstrated in this population, diltiazem should not be used in children.
STRICTLY FOLLOW YOUR DOCTOR’S PRESCRIPTION.
How Tildiem 60 mg tablets should be taken
Oral use.
Tablets should not be chewed, but swallowed whole with a glass of water.
How often Tildiem 60 mg tablets should be taken
Take this medicine at each of the 3 meals of the day, preferably at the beginning of the meal.
How long Tildiem 60 mg tablets should be taken
STRICTLY FOLLOW YOUR DOCTOR’S PRESCRIPTION.
If you take more Tildiem 60 mg tablets than you should If you take too high a dose, call your doctor immediately. If you forget to take Tildiem 60 mg tablets
Contact your doctor.


Like all medicines, Tildiem 60 mg tablets can cause side effects, although not everybody gets them.
In general, the frequency of side effects is classed as follows:
• very common (more than 1 in 10 patients),
• common (more than 1 in 100 patients and less than 1 in 10 patients),
• uncommon (more than 1 in 1,000 patients and less than 1 in 100 patients),
• rare (more than 1 in 10,000 patients and less than 1 in 1,000 patients),
• very rare (less than 1 in 10,000 patients).
Heart disorders:
• Common: certain heart conduction disorders, palpitations,
• Uncommon: poorly tolerated slow heart rate,
• Not known: congestive heart failure, other heart conduction disorders.
Vascular disorders:
These disorders are related to the way the medicine works and occur more readily in elderly subjects.Common: flushing,
• Uncommon: postural hypotension,
• Not known: inflammation of the small blood vessels (vasculitis).
Digestive disorders:
• Common: constipation, stomach ache, nausea, dyspepsia,
• Uncommon: vomiting, diarrhea,
• Rare: dry mouth,
• Not known: excessive enlargement of the gums.
Skin disorders:
• Common: erythema,
• Rare: hives,
• Not known:
- sudden swelling of the face and neck (angioedema) of allergic origin,
- various types of skin rash, such as erythema multiforme (in particular
Stevens-Johnson syndrome and toxic epidermal necrolysis), acute generalized
exanthematous pustulosis (mucous membrane damage or blister-like skin
reactions): stop treatment immediately and consult your doctor,
- skin reactions triggered by exposure to the sun or UV rays (photosensitization
reactions such as lichenoid keratosis on areas of exposed skin),

- sweating,
- erythema, sometimes with fever and/or desquamation,
- rash,
Liver disorders:
• Uncommon: increase in hepatic enzymes (generally temporary),
• Not known: clinical hepatitis reversible on treatment discontinuation.
Nervous system disorders:
• Common: headache, dizziness,
• Not known: extrapyramidal symptoms (disorder with rigidity, trembling, and
abnormal movements), generally reversible on treatment discontinuation.
Psychiatric disorders:
• Uncommon: nervousness, insomnia,
• Not known: mood changes (especially depression).
Reproductive system disorders:
• Not known: breast swelling in men, reversible on treatment discontinuation.
Blood and lymphatic system disorders:
• Not known: reduction in the number of platelets in the blood.
General disorders:
• Very common: swelling of the lower limbs,
• Common: faintness, fatigue.
If you notice any side effects not listed in this leaflet, or if any of the side effects becomes serious, please tell your doctor or pharmacist.

TILDIEM 60 mg, Tablet

Diltiazem hydrochloride 60 mg For the full list of excipient see section 6.1.

Tablet

Prevention of attacks of angina pectoris, particularly exertional angina, rest angina including variant
angina of Prinzmetal.


Adults:
The usual dosage is 1 x 60 mg tablet three times daily at the start of meals.
In severe cases, dosage can be increased to 4 tablets (i.e. 240 mg) or 6 tablets (i.e. 360 mg) daily.
Children:
As safety and efficacy have not been established, use of diltiazem is not recommended in children.
Elderly and patients with impaired hepatic or renal function:
The recommended starting dose is one tablet (60mg) twice daily. The heart rate should be measured regularly in these groups of patients and the dose should not be increased if the heart rate falls below 50 beats per minute.
Method of administration:
Oral use.
Tablets should not be chewed but swallowed with a little water.
Diltiazem should be used with caution in patients with kidney or liver failure (see section 4.4).


This medicine must never be used in the following situations: hypersensitivity to diltiazem or to any of the excipients sinus node dysfunction second- and third-degree atrioventricular block in patients without a pacemaker left ventricular failurewith pulmonary congestion severe bradycardia (heart rate 40 bpm) in combination with: o dantrolene administered by infusion o pimozide o cisapride o dihydroergotamine o ergotamine o nifedipine o sertindole.

Diltiazem hydrochloride is generally not recommended in the following situations: In combination with:
o esmolol (in patients with impaired left ventricular function)
o beta-blockers used in heart failure (bisoprolol, carvedilol, metoprolol, nebivolol)
o other beta-blockers o triazolam
o ivabradine In pregnant women or women who may be pregnant (see section 4.6) Monitoring should be carried out in patients with impaired left ventricular function, bradycardia
(due to risk of exacerbation), or first-degree atrioventricular block on the ECG (due to risk of exacerbation and, in exceptional cases, complete block). However, there are no special precautions for patients with bundle branch block. In elderly subjects and patientswith kidney or liver failure, diltiazem plasma concentrations may
be increased. Particular attention should be paid to contraindications and precautions for use. Close monitoring, particularly of heart rate and ECG should be performed at the beginning of treatment.

Diltiazem is likely to induce a decrease in blood pressure and significant bradycardia, particularly
in elderly subjects. Patients due to undergo general anesthesia should inform the anesthesiologist that they are taking
diltiazem. During general anesthesia, diltiazem generally triggers a moderate decrease in blood pressure and
systemic vascular resistance, as well as a slight slowing of heart rate. The vasodilation induced by anesthetics may be potentiated by diltiazem. Their dose must be adapted to the hemodynamic response. Diltiazem can be used safely in patients with chronic respiratory disorders. Calcium channel blockers such as diltiazem can be associated with mood disorders and, in
particular, depression. Like other calcium channel blockers, diltiazem has an inhibitory effect on intestinal motility.
Consequently, it must be used with caution in patients at risk of developing an intestinal obstruction. This medicine contains castor oil, which can cause digestive disorders (mild laxative effect,
diarrhea). This medicine contains lactose. Its use is not recommended in patients with galactose intolerance,
Lapp lactase deficiency or glucose and galactose malabsorption syndrome (rare hereditary diseases).


interaction Antiarrhythmic drugs
Many antiarrythmics have depressant effects on cardiac automaticity, conduction and contractility.
Combined use of antiarrhythmics of different classes can have a beneficial therapeutic effect, but is most often problematic, and requires close clinical monitoring and ECG monitoring. Combined use of antiarrythmics that induce torsades de pointes (amiodarone, disopyramide, quinidines, sotalol, etc.) is contraindicated.
Combined use of antiarrhythmics of the same class is not recommended, except in exceptional cases, due to the higher risk of undesirable cardiac effects.
Combination with drugs that have negative inotropic properties, bradycardia-inducing effects and/or medicines that slow atrioventricular conduction can be problematic and requires clinical and ECG monitoring (amiodarone, bepridil, cibenzoline, diltiazem, disopyramide, dofetilide, flecainide, hydroquinidine, ibutilide, lidocaine, mexiletine, propafenone, quinidine, sotalol, verapamil).
Drugs with bradycardia-inducing effects
Many drugs can induce bradycardia, particularly class Ia antiarrythmics, beta-blockers, certain class

II antiarrhythmics, certain calcium channel blockers, digitalis glycosides, pilocarpine, anticholinesterase agents, etc.
Contraindicated combinations
+ Cisapride
Increased risk of ventricular arrhythmias, particularly torsades de pointes.
+ Dantrolene infusion
Cases of fatal ventricular fibrillation are regularly observed in animals following IV administration of verapamil and dantrolene. Therefore, combination of a calcium channel blocker with dantrolene is potentially dangerous. However, some patients have received concomitant treatment with nifedipine and dantrolene without any problems.
+ Dihydroergotamine
Ergotism with possible necrosis of the extremities (inhibited hepatic metabolism of rye ergot alkaloids).
+ Ergotamine
Ergotism with possible necrosis of the extremities (inhibited hepatic metabolism of rye ergot alkaloids).
+ Nifedipine
Significant increase in nifedipine concentrations due to reduction of its hepatic metabolism by diltiazem, with risk of severe hypotension.
+ Pimozide
Increased risk of ventricular arrhythmias, particularly torsades de pointes.
+ Sertindole
Increased risk of ventricular arrhythmias, particularly torsades de pointes.
Inadvisable combinations
+ Beta-blockers (excluding esmolol)
Automaticity disorders (excessive bradycardia, sinus arrest), sinoatrial and atrioventricular conduction disorders, and heart failure.
This combination requires close clinical and ECG monitoring, particularly in elderly subjects and at the start of treatment.
+ Beta-blockers used in the treatment of heart failure (bisoprolol, carvedilol, metoprolol,
nebivolol)
Negative inotropic effect with a risk of decompensation of heart failure, automaticity disorders
(bradycardia, sinus arrest) and sinoatrial and atrioventricular conduction disorders.
+ Esmolol, in patients with impaired left ventricular function

Automaticity disorders (excessive bradycardia, sinus arrest), sinoatrial and atrioventricular conduction disorders and heart failure.
+ Ivabradine
Increased ivabradine plasma concentrations and undesirable effects, particularly those affecting the heart (diltiazem inhibits the hepatic metabolism of ivabradine), adding to the bradycardia-inducing effects of these drugs.
+ Triazolam
Increased triazolam plasma concentrations due to reduced hepatic metabolism, with increased sedation.
Combinations requiring precautions for use
+ Alfentanil
Increased respiratory depressant effect of the opiate analgesic due to reduced hepatic metabolism. The dose of the analgesic should be adjusted if used with diltiazem.
+ Amiodarone
Risk of bradycardia and atrioventricular block, particularly in elderly subjects. Clinical and ECG monitoring.
+ Enzyme-inducing antiepileptic drugs
Reduced plasma concentrations of the calcium channel blocker due to increased hepatic metabolism by the inducer.
Clinical monitoring is required, and, if necessary, calcium channel blocker dose adjustment during and after treatment with the inducer.
+ Atorvastatin
Increased risk of undesirable effects (concentration-dependent) such as rhabdomyolysis due to reduced hepatic metabolism of the cholesterol-lowering drug. Lower doses of the cholesterol- lowering drug or another statin not affected by this type of interaction should be used.
+ Baclofen
Increased risk of hypotension, particularly postural hypotension.
Monitoring of blood pressure and antihypertensive dose adjustment if necessary.
+ Buspirone
Increased buspirone plasma concentrations due to reduction of its hepatic metabolism by diltiazem, with an increase in undesirable effects.
Clinical monitoring and buspirone dose adjustment if necessary.

+ Dronedarone
Risk of bradycardia and atrioventricular block, particularly in elderly subjects. Furthermore, a slight increase in dronedarone concentrations due to a reduction of its metabolism by the calcium channel blocker.
Calcium channel blocker treatment should be initiated at the minimum recommended dosage and the doses adjusted on the basis of the ECG.
+ Esmolol, in patients with normal left ventricular function
Automaticity disorders (excessive bradycardia, sinus arrest), sinoatrial and atrioventricular conduction disorders and heart failure.
Clinical and ECG monitoring.
+ Immunosuppressants (ciclosporin, everolimus, sirolimus, tacrolimus)
Increased blood concentrations of the immunosuppressant due to reduced metabolism.
Assay of the blood concentrations of the immunosuppressant, monitoring of kidney function and dose adjustment during and after treatment.
+ Midazolam
Increased plasma midazolam concentrations due to reduced hepatic metabolism, with increased sedation.
Clinical monitoring and reduction of dosage during treatment with diltiazem.
+ Medicinal products likely to induce torsades de pointes (excluding pimozide, cisapride): class Ia antiarrhythmics (quinidine, hydroquinidine, disopyramide); class III antiarrhythmics (amiodarone, dofetilide, ibutilide, sotalol); certain neuroleptics: phenothiazines (chlorpromazine, cyamemazine, fluphenazine, levomepromazine), benzamides (amisulpride, sulpiride, sultopride, tiapride), butyrophenones (droperidol, haloperidol), pimozide, pipamperone, pipotiazine, zuclopenthixol; other: bepridil, diphemanil, erythromycin IV, halofantrine, lumefantrine, methadone, mizolastine, moxifloxacin, pentamidine, spiramycin IV, vincamine IV
Increased risk of ventricular arrhythmias, particularly torsades de pointes.
Clinical and ECG monitoring.
+ Rifampicin
Reduced plasma concentrations of the calcium channel blocker due to increased hepatic metabolism.
Clinical monitoring and, if necessary, calcium channel blocker dose adjustment during and after treatment with rifampicin.

+ Simvastatin
Increased risk of undesirable effects (dose-dependent) such as rhabdomyolysis due to reduced hepatic
metabolism of the cholesterol-lowering drug. The dosage of 20 mg/day of simvastatin should not be exceeded, or another statin not affected by this type of interaction should be administered. Combinations to be taken into account
+ Alpha-blockers for urologic use (alfuzosin, doxazosin, prazosin, tamsulosin, terazosin)
Increased hypotensive effect. Increased risk of postural hypotension.
+ Amifostine
Increased risk of hypotension, particularly postural.
+ Imipramine antidepressants
Increased risk of hypotension, particularly postural.
+ Alpha-blocking antihypertensives
Increased hypotensive effect. Increased risk of postural hypotension.
+ Other bradycardia-inducing drugs
Risk of excessive bradycardia (additive effects).
+ Clonidine, guanfacine
Automaticity disorders (atrioventricular conduction disorders due to additive undesirable effects on conduction).
+ Glucocorticoids (excluding hydrocortisone used as replacement therapy) and mineralocorticoids
Reduced antihypertensive effect (corticosteroid water and sodium retention).
+ Neuroleptics
Increased risk of hypotension, particularly postural.
+ Pilocarpine
Risk of excessive bradycardia (additive bradycardic effects).
+ Nitrates, nitrites and related drugs
Increased risk of hypotension, particularly postural.


lactation Pregnancy
Animal studies (rat, mouse, rabbit) have demonstrated teratogenic effects.
There are currently not enough data to evaluate the possible malformative and fetotoxic effect of diltiazem hydrochloride when administered during pregnancy. Therefore, use of diltiazem

hydrochloride is not recommended during pregnancy or in women of child-bearing potential not using contraceptives.
Lactation
Diltiazem is found at very low concentrations in breast milk.
However, breast-feeding should be avoided during treatment with diltiazem. If the use of diltiazem is necessary, the infant should be fed using an alternative method.

Classification of undesirable effects according to expected frequencies: very common (≥ 1/10);
common (≥ 1/100, < 1/10); uncommon (≥ 1/1,000, < 1/100); rare (≥ 1/10,000, < 1/1,000); very rare (< 1/10,000); not known (cannot be estimated from the available data).
Within each group frequency, undesirable effects are presented in order of decreasing seriousness.
Cardiac disorders
Common: atrioventricular block (which may be first, second or third degree; possible bundle-branch
block), palpitations. Uncommon: bradycardia.
Not known: sinoatrial block and congestive heart failure.
Vascular disorders
Effects caused by vasodilation (headache, flushing, and in particular, edema of the lower limbs) are
dose-dependent and are related to the pharmacological activity of the active substance. These occur more readily in elderly subjects.
Common: flushing.
Uncommon: postural hypotension.
Not known: vasculitis (including leukocytoclastic vasculitis).
Gastrointestinal disorders
Common: constipation, dyspepsia, epigastric pain, nausea.
Uncommon: vomiting, diarrhea. Rare: dry mouth.
Not known: gingival hyperplasia.

Skin and subcutaneous tissue disorders
Common: erythema.
Rare: urticaria.
Not known: angioedema, rash, erythema multiforme (particularly Stevens-Johnson syndrome and toxic epidermic necrolysis), exfoliative dermatitis, acute generalized exanthematous pustulosis, photosensitivity reaction (in particular lichenoid keratosis on areas of skin exposed to the sun), sweating, erythema, sometimes with fever and/or desquamation.
Hepatobiliary disorders
Uncommon: isolated, moderate, and generally transient increases in hepatic enzymes (AST, ALT,
LDH, alkaline phosphatase) have been observed during the initial treatment period. Not known: clinical hepatitis reversible on treatment discontinuation.
Nervous system disorders
Common: headache, dizziness.
Not known: extrapyramidal effects, generally reversible on treatment discontinuation.
Psychiatric disorders
Uncommon: nervousness, insomnia.
Not known: mood changes (especially depression).
Reproductive system disorders
Not known: gynecomastia generally reversible on treatment discontinuation.
General disorders and administration site conditions
Very common: swelling of the lower limbs. Common: malaise, asthenia.
Blood and lymphatic system disorders
Not known: thrombocytopenia.

The clinical picture for massive acute poisoning can include marked hypotension that can progress to
collapse, sinus bradycardia with or without isorhythmic dissociation and atrioventricular conduction disorders.
The measures to be taken in a hospital environment include: gastric lavage, osmotic diuresis. Conduction disorders may benefit from temporary electrosystolic pacing.
The antidotes proposed are: atropine, adrenaline, vasopressor substances, positive inotropic and chronotropic agents, glucagon and calcium gluconate by infusion.


SELECTIVE CALCIUM ANTAGONIST WITH DIRECT CARDIAC EFFECTS / BENZOTHIAZEPINE DERIVATIVES
(C08DB01: cardiovascular system)
Diltiazem slows calcium entry through membranes in vascular myocardial muscle fibre and smooth
muscle fibre and thus decreases the quantity of intracellular calcium reaching contractile proteins. In animals
Diltiazem increases coronary flow without causing the phenomenon of coronary steal. It acts on the small coronary arteries, on the large arterial trunks, on the collateral arteries. This vasodilator effect, which is exerted moderately on peripheral systemic arterial regions, is observed at doses which are not negatively inotropic.
In humans
Diltiazem increases coronary flow by reducing resistance.
Due to its moderate bradycardiac action and its moderate reduction in systemic arterial resistance, diltiazem reduces the cardiac workload.
No negative inotropic effect has been demonstrated on a healthy myocardium. Diltiazem induces a moderate slowing in heart rate and can have a depressant effect on a diseased sinus node. It slows down atrioventricular conduction, with a risk of atrioventricular block. Diltiazem has no effect on hisian and infra-hisian conduction.
The clinical efficacy of diltiazem has been demonstrated during controlled trials in all forms of angina.
On effort, the following have been observed: an improvement in performance,

a reduction in the amplitude of ST-segment depression (maximum at constant load, at constant HR and at constant double product).
Its remarkable activity in variant angina of Prinzmetal also demonstrates its marked protective effect against coronary spasm.


In humans, diltiazem demonstrates rapid intestinal absorption. It appears in the blood 30 minutes
after oral administration.
The peak plasma concentration is reached between the 3rd and the 4th hour. The apparent plasma half- life is 4 to 8 hours. Diltiazem is bound to plasma proteins in a proportion of 80-85%. Diltiazem is strongly metabolised by the liver; its main active metabolite is desacetyldiltiazem. Elimination is biliary (65%) and urinary (35%).
Only 0.2 to 0.4% unchanged diltiazem is found in the urine.
In general, there is a significant relationship between the dose absorbed and the plasma concentration. Any increase in this dose is followed by a proportional increase in the plasma level, suggesting the absence of any saturation phenomenon.
At constant dosage, the plasma levels are relatively constant.
There is a relationship between plasma concentration and efficacy. The plasma levels required to obtain satisfactory efficacy are, on average, between 70 and 200 ng/ml. It would appear the more severe the angina, the higher these plasma levels must be.
The pharmacokinetic profile is not modified in the event of impaired kidney function.
Plasma concentrations in the elderly and in subjects with impaired liver and kidney function are, on average, higher than in young subjects.
Diltiazem and its metabolites are barely dialysable
