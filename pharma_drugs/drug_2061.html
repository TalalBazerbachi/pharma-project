Resochin contains the active substance chloroquine phosphate
Resochin Tablets is used for
 treatment and prevention of malaria.
 Resochin Tablets act on all four malaria germs that cause disease in
humans. The generally accepted guidelines on the appropriate use of
medicines for treating malaria (antimalarials) are to be observed.
 chronic rheumatoid arthritis including juvenile chronic arthritis.

Explanations
Malaria (marsh fever, tertian fever) is a severe, feverish disease whose germs
are transmitted by mosquitoes. Chronic rheumatoid arthritis and juvenile
chronic rheumatoid arthritis are connective tissue disorders that generally
mainly affect the joints, but internal organs may also be affected. Systemic
lupus erythematosus (lupus) is a connective tissue disorder affecting the skin
and also internal organs


Some people being treated with Resochin tablets may develop mental health
problems such as unreasonable thoughts, hallucinations, feeling confused,
aggression, paranoia, feeling depressed, or having thoughts of harming or
killing themselves. This can also affect people who have never had such
problems before. If you or others notice any of these side effects (see section
4), seek medical advice immediately.

if you are allergic to chloroquine phosphate or other 4-aminoquinolines
(including chloroquinoline phosphate, the active substance of Resochin
Tablets) or any of the other ingredients of this medicine listed in section
6,

 if you have a blood formation disorder,
 if you have glucose-6-phosphate dehydrogenase deficiency (a rare
disorder with possible destruction of red blood cells),
 if you suffer from myasthenia gravis (severe muscle weakness) or

Warnings and precautions
Talk to your doctor or pharmacist before taking Resochin Tablets.
The insensitivity (so-called chloroquine resistance) of one malaria pathogen
(Plasmodium falciparum) is well documented. These data should be taken
into consideration before starting treatment with chloroquine, to ensure that
the treatment will be successful.
Simultaneous treatment with Resochin Tablets and other medicines for
treating rheumatoid arthritis (e.g. penicillamine), substances that have
a toxic effect on the liver (e.g. isoniazid, amiodarone, carbamazepine,
phenytoin, phenothiazines and ketoconazole) or MAO inhibitors (medicines
that inhibit a certain active substance in the body, e.g. tranylcypromine and
selegiline) should be avoided (see section 2. “Other medicines and Resochin
Tablets”).
Damage to the retina (retinopathy)
Before the start of prolonged treatment, an eye examination will be carried
out and is to be repeated at 3-monthly intervals. At the first signs of retinal
damage (inability to see the colour red), treatment must be discontinued.
Chloroquine should be administered only to children who are able to
undergo a colour vision test.
Permanent visual disturbances caused by Resochin-induced retinopathy are
possible during long-term treatment at high dosages. Retinal examinations
are recommended for patients receiving long-term therapy with Resochin.
Cumulative total doses of 1 g chloroquine per kg body weight or 50 to 100g
total dose can lead to retinal damage. In rare cases, retinopathy has been
induced by dosages recommended for malaria prevention. Within the context
of malaria prevention with Resochin, the onset of retinopathy is therefore
relevant only in long-term use (> 5 years).
Impaired liver function
As Resochin is known to accumulate in the liver, patients with impaired liver
function may need to take a lower amount of Resochin Tablets.
Impaired kidney function
Resochin is partly excreted via the kidneys. For this reason, patients with
impaired kidney function may need to take a lower amount of Resochin
Tablets.
Kidney failure
Due to a lack of adequate experience, administration of Resochin in patients
with kidney failure (decrease in a certain laboratory value (creatinine
clearance) below 10 mL/min) is not to be recommended.
Nervous system
In patients suffering from epilepsy (seizures, muscle spasms with
unconsciousness), regular medical care is required.
Blood count
The blood count must also be checked before the start of long-term
treatment and checks are to be repeated at intervals of two months.
Metabolic disorders
Resochin should not be used in patients with porphyria (impaired production
of the red pigment in blood), as the use of chloroquine may cause symptoms to worsen.
Skin conditions
Resochin should not be used in patients with psoriasis, as the use of
chloroquine may cause symptoms to worsen.
QT prolongation
Resochin Tablets may cause heart rhythm disorders in some patients:
caution should be taken when using Resochin Tablets if you were born with
or have a family history of a prolonged QT interval, if you have acquired QT
prolongation (seen on ECG, electrical recording of the heart), if you have any
heart disorders or have a history of heart attack (myocardial infarction), if
you have a salt imbalance in the blood (especially low level of potassium or
magnesium, see section 2. “Other medicines and Resochin Tablets”).
If you experience palpitations or an irregular heartbeat during treatment,
you should inform your doctor immediately. The risk of heart problems may
increase with increases in the dose. Therefore, the recommended dose should
be followed.
Cardiotoxicity
At therapeutic doses and in the event of an acute overdose, chloroquine may
cause damage to the heart (cardiotoxicity).
Therapeutic doses may cause cardiomyopathy (a disease that weakens the
heart muscle and strength of the heartbeat). Cardiomyopathy may lead to
heart failure, which may be fatal in some cases.
In the event of an acute overdose, chloroquine may cause heart failure (the
heart muscle cannot pump strongly enough to supply the whole body with
blood) and serious heart rhythm disorders (see section 3. “If you take more
Resochin Tablets than you should”).
If you develop an irregular heartbeat during treatment with chloroquine, tell
your doctor immediately.
Hypoglycaemia
Resochin can cause a fall in the blood sugar level. Please ask your doctor
for information about signs and symptoms of low blood sugar. It may be
necessary to have your blood sugar level checked.
Long-term treatment in women of childbearing potential
Female patients taking Resochin Tablets in long-term use should use reliable
contraceptive protection during treatment and for 3 months thereafter (see
section 2. “Pregnancy and breast-feeding”).
Children and adolescents
There should be no long-term administration for the prevention of malaria
in children.
If Resochin is taken on a long-term basis, precautionary measures should
be taken in relation to the total dose (see section 3. “How to take Resochin
Tablets”).
Other medicines and Resochin Tablets
Tell your doctor or pharmacist if you are taking/using, have recently taken/
used or might take/use any other medicines.
Medicines known to affect the rhythm of your heart. This includes medicines
used for abnormal heart rhythm (antiarrhythmics), for depression (tricyclic
antidepressants), for psychiatric disorders (antipsychotics), for bacterial
infections or against malaria (e.g. halofantrine).

affect the way these other medicines work:
R Reduced antibody formation when vaccinating against rabies with the

R Increase in the plasma digoxin concentration with glycoside poisoning

R Increased effect of folic acid antagonists (methotrexate, an active

R Reduced effect of neostigmine and pyridostigmine bromide (muscle

R Increase in the plasma concentration of ciclosporin (active substance
used to suppress the body’s immune system),
R Reduced absorption of ampicillin (an antibacterial agent; ampicillin
should be given at least 2 hours after administration of Resochin),
R The simultaneous use of praziquantel (agent to treat infestation with
flukes and tapeworms or lung and liver flukes) together with chloroquine
may result in a reduced concentration of praziquantel. The mechanism
and clinical significance of this interaction are unclear.
Resochin with alcohol
While taking Resochin Tablets, you should preferably not drink alcohol.
Pregnancy, breast-feeding and fertility
If you are pregnant or breast-feeding, think you may be pregnant or are
planning to have a baby, ask your doctor or pharmacist for advice before
taking this medicine.
Use in chronic rheumatoid arthritis and systemic lupus erythematosus
Pregnancy
Resochin Tablets must not be used in pregnancy, as they may cause organ
damage in the child. Pregnancy must be ruled out before the start of
treatment. During treatment and for three months afterwards, effective
contraceptive protection must be maintained.
Breast-feeding
Resochin Tablets must not be used during breast-feeding, as no adequate
experience is available and chloroquine phosphate, the active substance of
Resochin Tablets, passes into human milk.
Fertility
No data are available.
Use for the treatment and prevention of malaria
Pregnancy
During pregnancy, Resochin Tablets may be given only after a careful
assessment of the benefits and risks by your doctor. As a rule, he/she will
decide to use Resochin Tablets, as the malaria infection itself will harm the
unborn child.
Before starting use for the treatment and prevention of malaria, it must be
established whether the woman is pregnant. Once pregnancy has been ruled
out, effective contraceptive protection must be maintained during treatment
and for three months thereafter.
Breast-feeding
Women must not breast-feed (see above).
Fertility
No data are available.
Driving and using machines
Do not drive or use machines if you notice any side effects of Resochin
Tablets, such as headache, dizziness, drowsiness, states of confusion,
impairment of sharp vision and flickering blind spots before the eyes. These
may occur even with correct use. This applies particularly at the start of
treatment and in conjunction with alcohol.


Always take Resochin Tablets exactly as your doctor has told you. Check with
your doctor or pharmacist if you are not sure.
Total dose and general information for adults, children and adolescents
The total amount of 1 g chloroquine per kilogram of body weight (or a total
of about 50 - 100 g) taken over time can lead to retinal damage. For this
reason, the total dose should not exceed 50 g chloroquine. For children, the
normal gain in weight during the growth of the child should be considered
when calculating the maximum duration of treatment.
While considering the total dose, the total duration of treatment can
vary, depending on the patient’s body weight and specific prescription
requirements. It is recommended, for the initial prescription, that the total
duration of treatment is calculated for you individually and that it is ensured
that the duration of treatment is recalculated each time there is a change
in the prescription parameters. You should be informed of the individually
calculated total duration of treatment. Special care should be taken not to
exceed the total duration of treatment.
Thereafter, prevention must be continued with a medicine not containing
chloroquine.
Please note that, in children, the limit for the total amount taken may
already have been reached after this medicine has been taken continuously
for 3 years.
In certain malaria zones, twice the total weekly amount is advisable for
short stays, with tablet-taking being spread evenly over 2 days. Please follow
your doctor’s recommendations.

Treatment of chronic rheumatoid arthritis and systemic lupus
erythematosus:
The Resochin dose you require will be individually established for you by
your treating doctor. Due to the risk of eye disease affecting the retina,
the daily dose for long-term treatment should be no more than 4 mg
chloroquine phosphate (the active substance of Resochin Tablets) per
kilogram of body weight.
The success of treatment cannot be evaluated until after at least 4 to
12 weeks. Treatment will be discontinued by your doctor if there is no
improvement after 6 months.
Adults
A typical starting dose for adults is 250 mg of chloroquine phosphate
(equivalent to 155 mg of chloroquine) once daily.
Use in children and adolescents
The daily dose for children is 4 mg of chloroquine phosphate (equivalent to
2.5 mg of chloroquine) per kg of body weight. Due to the risk of retinopathy,
this dose should not be exceeded. Chloroquine should be administered only

to children who are able to undergo a colour vision test.
Dosage for patients with impaired liver or kidney function
Patients with impaired liver or kidney function may need a lower dose of

Method of administration
Please swallow the tablets whole with sufficient liquid (preferably a glass of

Duration of administration
Your doctor will decide on the duration of treatment.
There should be no long-term administration for the prevention of malaria

If you have the impression that the effect of Resochin Tablets is too strong or
too weak, talk to your doctor or pharmacist.
If you take more Resochin Tablets than you should
If you accidentally take double the single dose of Resochin Tablets, this
will have no effect on your further doses, i.e. you should continue to take
Resochin Tablets as normal.
If you accidentally take more Resochin Tablets than you should, tell a doctor
straight away. The following effects may happen: heart problems – may lead
to an irregular heartbeat.
If significantly excessive amounts of Resochin Tablets are taken, this can
lead to paralysing effects on the heart, circulation and breathing. Early signs
of intoxication are headache, visual disturbances and irregular heartbeat,
followed by seizures. If such signs of illness occur, you must be given
medical care immediately and admitted to hospital. Inform the nearest
available doctor as quickly as possible.
If a child has swallowed Resochin Tablets in an unsupervised moment, the
child must be made to vomit immediately and the nearest available doctor
must be notified.
Until the doctor arrives, the following measures are recommended:
A small child should be given as much water to drink as possible or,
even better, fruit juice diluted with water. He/she should then be placed
over the knee of an adult with the head and face downwards and the
abdomen gently compressed. Next, the child should be made to vomit by
irritating the back of its throat with a finger.
 An older child should rapidly drink warm salt water (1 to 2 tablespoons
of salt in 1 glass of water). He/she should also be made to vomit by
irritating the back of his/her throat with a finger.
R Drinking and vomiting should be repeated until clear liquid is brought
up.
If you forget to take Resochin Tablets
For the prevention and treatment of malaria, please make up for a forgotten
dose if you realise that you have missed a dose shortly after your dosing
time. Similarly, if the next dosing time is nearer than the missed dosing time,
please make up for the forgotten dose, but postpone taking your next dose
for a while, so that there is a more even interval between doses.
In the treatment of chronic rheumatoid arthritis and systemic lupus
erythematosus, if you do not realise you have forgotten your dose until the
next day, do not make up for this dose. Simply continue to take Resochin
Tablets as normal.
If you stop taking Resochin Tablets
If you experience unpleasant side effects, please talk to your doctor
immediately about what can be done to combat them and whether any
other medicines are available for your treatment. Please do not stop taking
this medicine without consulting a doctor under any circumstances.

Cumulative dose and general information for adults, children and adolescents

 

Total cumulative dosages of 1 g chloroquine per kg body weight (BW) or 50‑100 g total dose may lead to retinal damage. A total cumulative dose of 50 g chloroquine should therefore not be exceeded in adults. In children, the maximum cumulative dose should not exceed 1 g per kg body weight, and the usual weight gain during the growth of the child should be taken into account for the maximum duration of treatment (see section 4.4).

 

Taking into account this total cumulative dose, the total duration of use may vary according to the patient's body weight and the individual dose. It is recommended that the total duration of use for the patient be calculated when the product is first prescribed and that recalculation of the duration of use in the event that the treatment parameters change in any way be ensured. The patient should be informed of the individually calculated total duration of use. Particular care should be taken to ensure that the total duration of use is not exceeded.

 

After the individually calculated maximum duration is reached, prophylaxis with chloroquine must be stopped and, if necessary, continued with other active substances.

Treatment of rheumatoid arthritis and systemic lupus erythematosus

 

Adults

The dosage is determined individually and, if necessary, adjusted in the course of therapy according to tolerability and effect. A typical starting dose in adults is 250 mg chloroquine phosphate (equivalent to 155 mg chloroquine) once daily.

Because of the risk of retinopathy during long‑term therapy, no more than 4 mg chloroquine phosphate (equivalent to 2.5 mg chloroquine) per kg body weight should be given per day.

At least 4 to 12 weeks of treatment are required before the response to treatment can be assessed. Treatment must be discontinued if there has been no improvement after 6 months.

 

Paediatric population

The daily dose for children is 4 mg chloroquine phosphate (equivalent to 2.5 mg chloroquine) per kg body weight. This dose should not be exceeded because of the risk of retinopathy. Chloroquine should be used only in children who are capable of completing a colour vision test.

Treatment must be discontinued if there has been no improvement after 6 months.

 

Patients with hepatic and renal impairment

Patients with hepatic or renal impairment may require a dose adjustment (see section 4.4).

The film‑coated tablets must be swallowed whole with sufficient liquid

Suicidal behavior and psychiatric illnesses

Suicidal behavior and psychiatric illness have been observed in patients treated with chloroquine (see section 4.8), including in patients with no history of psychiatric illness. Patients should be advised to seek medical advice immediately if they develop psychiatric symptoms during treatment

 

Retinopathy

Before the start of more prolonged oral therapy, an eye examination by an ophthalmologist must be carried out, which must be repeated at 3‑monthly intervals whilst treatment is ongoing. At the first signs of retinopathy (loss of red colour vision), treatment must be discontinued immediately.

The main recommended methods for retinopathy screening are automated visual field testing and (spectral domain) optical coherence tomography (SD‑OCT). If a colour vision test is the only available screening method for retinopathy, chloroquine should be used only in children who are capable of completing the colour vision test.

Irreversible impairment of vision caused by Resochin‑induced retinopathy is well documented in cases of long‑term therapy with high dosages. Retinal examinations are recommended for patients receiving long-term therapy with Resochin. Total cumulative doses of 1 g per kg body weight chloroquine or of 50 to 100 g total dose are associated with the occurrence of retinal damage (see section 4.8). Retinopathy has rarely, if ever, been caused by dosages recommended for malaria prophylaxis. In connection with prophylaxis, retinopathy is thus only of importance in cases of long‑term use (> 5 years) of Resochin.

 

Hepatic impairment

Because Resochin is known to accumulate in the liver, the dose may need to be adjusted for patients with hepatic impairment.

 

Renal impairment

Resochin is partly excreted via the kidneys. The dose may therefore need to be adjusted for patients with renal impairment.

 

Kidney failure

Owing to a lack of data, administration of Resochin cannot be recommended in patients with kidney failure (creatinine clearance < 10 mL/min).

 

Nervous system

Patients who suffer from epilepsy should receive regular medical care.

 

Blood count

A blood count check must be carried out before the start of long‑term treatment and repeated at two‑monthly intervals.

 

Metabolic disorders

Resochin should not be used in patients with porphyria because chloroquine use may lead to a deterioration in symptoms (see section 4.8).

 

Skin disorders

Resochin should not be used in patients with psoriasis because chloroquine use may lead to a deterioration in symptoms (see section 4.8).

 

Prolongation of the QTc interval

In some patients, chloroquine caused prolongation of the QTc interval.

Resochin should be used with caution in patients with congenital or proven acquired QT prolongation and/or known risk factors for prolongation of the QT interval, such as:

-          heart disease, e.g. heart failure, myocardial infarction

-          proarrhythmic conditions such as bradycardia (< 50 bpm)

-          previous history of ventricular dysrhythmias

-          untreated hypokalaemia and/or hypomagnesaemia

-          and during concomitant use of substances that prolong the QT interval (see section 4.5)

as this may be associated with an increased risk of ventricular arrhythmias, sometimes with a fatal outcome.

The magnitude of QT prolongation may increase with increasing medicinal product concentrations. The recommended dose should therefore not be exceeded (see also sections 4.8 and 4.9).

If signs of a cardiac arrhythmia occur during treatment with Resochin, the treatment should be terminated and an ECG performed.

 

Cardiomyopathy and cardiotoxicity

Chloroquine may lead to cardiotoxicity in therapeutic dosages and following an acute overdose.

Cases of cardiomyopathy leading to heart failure, sometimes with a fatal outcome, have been reported in patients receiving chloroquine therapy (see sections 4.8 and 4.9). If signs and symptoms of cardiomyopathy occur during treatment with Resochin, the treatment should be terminated. Various degrees of atrioventricular block have also been reported at therapeutic dosages.

Following an acute overdose, chloroquine may lead to acute heart failure and severe arrhythmias (see section 4.9 "Overdose").

 

Hypoglycaemia

Chloroquine has been shown to cause severe hypoglycaemia, including loss of consciousness, regardless of whether or not patients are being treated with antidiabetic agents. This can be life‑threatening. Patients being treated with chloroquine should be warned of the risk of hypoglycaemia and the associated clinical signs and symptoms. Patients who display clinical symptoms of hypoglycaemia during treatment with chloroquine should have their blood glucose levels checked and the need for an adjustment of their treatment reviewed.

 

Long‑term use in women of childbearing potential

Female patients taking Resochin tablets for long‑term use should use a reliable method of contraception during treatment and for 3 months thereafter (see section 4.6).

 

Long‑term use in children

Resochin should not be used for long‑term prophylaxis in children due to insufficient experience.

During administration of Resochin, precautions should be taken with regard to the cumulative dose in long‑term use (see section 4.2).

Pregnancy

Resochin must not be used in pregnancy because it crosses the placental barrier and can cause organ damage in the foetus. Pregnancy must be ruled out before treatment. An effective method of contraception must be used during treatment and for three months thereafter.

 

Breastfeeding

Resochin must not be used during breastfeeding because approx. 2 to 4% of the active substance passes into breast milk and accumulation in the infant is likely because of the long half‑life of Resochin. Although there have been no reports to date of harmful effects on the infant, women must not breastfeed because of the insufficient experience.

 

Fertility

Data on fertility are not available.

Acute overdose

Acute poisoning with 3 to 8 g chloroquine phosphate (equivalent to 2 to 5 g chloroquine) can be fatal within 1 to 3 hours as a result of the paralysing effect on the heart, circulation and breathing. Symptoms of the prodromal stage of chloroquine intoxication are headache, visual disturbances and cardiac arrhythmias. A drop in blood pressure is followed by the development of shock with loss of consciousness and seizures, followed shortly by respiratory and cardiac arrest.

An acute Resochin overdose is capable of causing QT prolongation, ventricular arrhythmias and torsades de pointes tachycardia.

 

In cases of severe intoxication, widening of the QRS complex, bradyarrhythmias, nodal rhythm, QT prolongation, atrioventricular block, ventricular tachycardia, torsades de pointes and ventricular fibrillation can occur.

 

Chronic overdose

Chronic overdose can lead to fatal cardiomyopathy. If signs of cardiomyopathy occur, treatment with chloroquine should be discontinued immediately. Toxic chloroquine‑induced cardiomyopathy can be reversible if it is detected at an early stage (see section 4.4). Chronic overdose can also lead to complete AV block.

 

Therapeutic measures in the event of overdose

There is no known antidote. First, an attempt should be made, if it can be done promptly, to eliminate the toxin immediately by means of gastric lavage. Respiratory and circulatory support (epinephrine) must then be initiated early on. The seizures can be suppressed with benzodiazepines (diazepam), phenobarbital, if necessary by means of peripheral muscle relaxants with artificial ventilation. Haemodialysis is not suitable for treatment. It may be necessary to compensate for severe hypokalaemia.


Pharmacotherapeutic group: Antimalarials, ATC code: P01BA01

 

Chloroquine belongs to the 4‑aminoquinoline group of substances that are suitable for malaria treatment and prophylaxis. The antimalarial effect of chloroquine is attributable partly to the binding of chloroquine to porphyrins, which leads to the destruction/inhibition of asexual forms of non‑resistant plasmodia in the red blood cells and disrupts the development of sexual forms (gametocytes) in P. ovale, P. vivax, P. malariae and immature forms of P. falciparum. In combination with emetine derivatives, chloroquine is also effective against extraintestinal amoebiasis.

Chloroquine is an active substance that has a positive influence on the disease course of rheumatoid arthritis during long‑term treatment and can induce remission. Chloroquine displays no effects in most animal experimental models of inflammation. The antirheumatic effect of this substance might be due to immunosuppressive activity. Efficacy has also been proven in systemic lupus erythematosus


Absorption

Chloroquine is rapidly and almost completely absorbed following oral administration. The maximum concentrations in the blood are reached after 3 h. Plasma levels above 9.6 µg/L (> 30 nmol/L), i.e. approximately 12.8 to 32 µg/L, are needed for malaria prophylaxis. Plasma levels of 96 to 192 µg/L are needed for the treatment of malaria.

Pharmacokinetics of chloroquine (µg/L)

 

Distribution

Chloroquine is distributed in the tissues in different concentrations. The volume of distribution is large. In whole blood, most of the chloroquine is bound to cellular elements. The portion found in the plasma is 50 to 60% bound to plasma proteins. In the course of treatment, chloroquine accumulates in the organs. The concentration found in the heart, lungs, kidneys and liver is more than 10 times, in parenchymatous cells 100 to 500 times and in pigmented cells up to 1000 times the concentration in the plasma.

 

Biotransformation

Metabolism takes place in the liver. The main metabolite is monodesethyl-chloroquine, which also still has antimalarial activity. It is excreted via the bile and kidneys.

 

Elimination

40 to 70% of chloroquine is excreted unchanged via the kidneys.

It is eliminated only slowly from the deep compartments. The half‑life, calculated from a multicompartment system, is stated as 30 to 60 days.


No regularly conducted studies of systemic toxicity, genotoxicity, carcinogenicity and embryotoxicity are available. Chloroquine is teratogenic in rats (various malformations). In another study, chloroquine induced eye defects in 47% of prenatally exposed rats.

 

Electrophysiological in vitro studies show that chloroquine is a multi‑ion channel blocker that influences the major cardiac depolarisation and repolarisation flows at IC50 concentrations in the range of 0.22 to 20 µmol/L, from which a risk of QT prolongation/cardiotoxicity can be inferred (see section 4.4).
