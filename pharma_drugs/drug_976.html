Neomox Powder For Oral Suspension 125mgj5 ml.

Each 5 ml of the constituted suspension contains 125 mg of Amoxicillin as Amoxicillin Trihydrate BP.

Powder for oral suspension

4.1. Theraoeutic indications:
Upper respiratory tract infections: Pharyngitis, laryngitis, tonsillitis, sinusitis and ottis
media.
Lower respiratory tract infections: Acute and chronic bronchitis, bronchopneumonia,
pneumonia, pulmonary abscesses and bronchiectasis.
Genitourinary tract infections: Cystitis, urethritis, pyelitis, pyelonephritis, nephritis,
prostatis, salpingitis, orchitis, gonorrhea.
Gastrointestinal infections: Enterocholitis, cholecystis, peritonitis.
Obstetrical and gynecological infections: Gonococcal vaginitis, septicabortion, septic
endometrisis and puerperal fever.
Other infections: Skin and soft tissue infections, abscesses, carbuncles and boils.


Adults: 250-750 mg every 8 hours depending on the severity of infection.
Children: 25-75 mgjkgjday in divided doses in every 8 hours depending on the severity of
infection.
Dosage recommendations for adults with impaired renal function: Patients with
impaired renal function do not generally require a reduction in dose unless the impairment
is severe. Patients with a glomerular filtration rate (GMR) of 10-30 mlfmin should receive
500 mg or 250 mg every 12 hours, depending upon the severity of the infection.

Patients with a less than 10 ml/min GMRshould receive 500 mg to 250 mg every 24 hours
depending on the severity of the infection. Hemodialysis patients should receive 500 mg to
250 mg every 24 hours depending on the severity of the infection. They should receive an
additional dose both during and at the end of dialysis.
There is currently no dosing requirements for paediatric patients with impaired renal
function.
Method of reconstitution (Powder for Oral Suspension) :Shake the bottle to loosen the
powder. Add water upto the mark on the label, invert bottle and shake until all powder is
dispersed, then slowly add more water upto the mark, keep tightly closed.


Neomox is contraindicated in patients with - 1. A previous history of hypersensitivity to penicillins. Attention should be paid to possible cross sensitivity with other beta-Iactum antibiotics. Eg cephalosporins. 2. A viral infection called infectious mononucleosis or glandular fever. 3. A blood disease called acute lymphatic leukaemia.

Serious and occasionally fatal hypersensitivity (anaphylactic) reactions have been reported
with patients on penicillin therapy. Although anaphylaxis is frequent in following parenteral
therapy, it has occurred in patients on oral penicillins. These reactions are more likely to
appear in individuals with a history of hypersensitivity to beta lactum antibiotics.
Erythematous rashes have been associated with glandular fever in patients receiving
amoxicillin.
Pseudomembranous colitis has been reported with nearly all anti bacterial agents including
amoxicillin and may range from mild to life threatening.
Prolonged use may occasionally result in overgrowth of non susceptible organisms.
At high doses relative to urinary output, adequate fluid intake and urinary output must be
maintained to minimise the possibility of amoxicillin crystalluria.
Dosage should be adjusted in patients with renal impairment.


Amoxicillinmay reduce the efficacy of oral contraceptives and hence patients should be
warned accordingly. Concurrent administration of allopurinol during treatment with amoxicillin can increase the
likelihood of allergic skin reactions.
Prolongation of prothrombin time has been reported rarely in patients receiving amoxicillin.
Therefore, appropriate monitoring should be undertaken when anticoagulants are prescribed
concurrently.
Probenecid decreases the renal tubular secretion of amoxicillin. Concurrent use of
amoxicillin and probenecid may result in increased and prolonged blood levels of amoxicillin.
When testing for the presence of glucose in urine with chemical methods, the high urinary
concentration of amoxicillin may lead to false positive readings. Hence it is recommended
that glucose tests based on enzymatic glucose oxidase reactions be used.


Though suitability of amoxicillin in pregnancy has been documented, amoxicillin should be
considered when the potential benefits outweigh the risks associated with treatment.
Amoxicillin may be given during lactation. Caution should be exercised when amoxicillin is
administered to nursing woman as trace quantities are excreted in breast milk.


Amoxicillin is generally well tolerated and side effects are mainly due to sensitivity
phenomena.
Gastrointestinal: Nausea, vomiting, diarrhea and hemorrhagic pseudomembranous colitis.
Hypersensitivity reactions: Serum sickness like reactions, erythematous maculopapular
rashes, erythema multiforme, Stevens-Johnson syndrome, exfoliative dermatitis, toxic
epidermal necrolysis, acute generalized exanthematous pustulosis, hypersensitivity
vasculitis and urticaria have been reported.
Liver: A moderate rise in AST (SGOT) and/or ALT (SGPT) has been noted, but the
significance of this finding is unknown. Hepatic dysfunction including cholestatic jaundice,
hepatic cholestasis and acute cytolytic hepatitis have been reported.
Hemic and Lymphatic: Anemia, including hemolytic anemia, thrombocytopenia,
thrombocytopenic purpura, eosinophilia, leukopenia and agranulocytosis have been reported
during therapy with penicillins. These reactions are reversible on discontinuation of
therapy.
Central nervous system: CNS side effects are rare. These include reversible hyperactivity,
agitation, anxiety, insomnia, confusion, convulsions, behavioural changes and/or dizziness.

In cases of overdosage, discontinue medications, treat symptomatically and institute
supportive measures as required. If the overdosage is very recent and there is no
contraindication, an attempt at emesis or other means of removal of drug from the stomach
must be performed. Renal impairment appears to be reversible with cessation of drug
administration. Amoxicillin may be removed from circulation by hemodialysis.


Amoxicillin is bactericidal. Neomox is a semi-synthetic penicillin with a rapid onset of action
and broad spectrum of bactericidal activity against both Gram-positive and Gram-negative
organisms. It acts by inhibiting the cell wall synthesis.
Amoxicillin is inactivated by penicillinase. Penicillinase-producing strains of Staphylococcus
aureus and Gram negative organisms (e.g. Escherichia coli, Proteus, Klebsiella) are
resistant.
Complete cross-resistance occurs with ampicillin and amoxicillin.


Neomox is acid stable and is rapidly and almost completely absorbed from the gastrointestinal
tract. Almost 90% of the oral dose is absorbed. Presence of food in the stomach
does not appear to diminish absorption of amoxicillin; hence amoxicillin can be administered
with food. Peak plasma concentration is reached at 1-2 hours.
Amoxicillin is widely distributed at varying concentrations in the body tissues and fluids with
the exception of brain and spinal fluid, except when meninges are inflamed. It penetrates
well into mucus, sputum and middle ear fluid. The half-life of amoxicillin is 1 to 1.5 hours.
Amoxicillin is mainly excreted in the urine unchanged, about 60% of oral dose being
excreted in 6 hours.
