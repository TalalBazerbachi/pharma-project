Chloraseptic lozenges are used as Oral anaesthetic/analgesic.
Temporarily relieves occasional minor irritation, pain, sore throat and sore mouth.
Chloraseptic lozenges contain Benzocaine 6 mg & Menthol 10 mg.
If you have any questions about how Chloraseptic works or why this medicine has been prescribed to you, ask your doctor.


Allergy Alert: Do not use this product if you have a history of allergy to local anesthetics such as procaine, butacaine, benzocaine or other “caine” anesthetics.
Sore Throat Warning: Severe or persistent sore throat or sore throat accompanied by high fever, headache, nausea and vomiting may be serious.
Consult a doctor promptly. Do not use more than 2 days unless directed by a doctor.
Stop use and ask a dentist or doctor if:
• sore mouth symptoms do not improve in 7 days
• irritation, pain or redness persists or worsens
• swelling, rash or fever develops
Other medicines and Chloraseptic lozenges
Interaction with other medicinal products and other forms of interaction not expected.
Pregnancy and Lactation
Chloraseptic Sore Throat Lozenges are not recommended during pregnancy or breastfeeding.
Ask a health care professional before use.
Driving and using machines
This product is not expected to affect your ability to drive and use machines.


Always take this medicine exactly as your doctor or pharmacist has told you. Check with your doctor or pharmacist if you are not sure.
Adults and children 6 years of age and over:
Take 1 lozenge every 2 hours as needed. Allow lozenges to dissolve slowly in the mouth.
Children under 6 years of age:
Consult a doctor or dentist.
If you take more Chloraseptic lozenges than you should
If you have accidentally taken more Chloraseptic lozenges than prescribed, tell your doctor or pharmacist or go to the hospital emergency unit at once. You may require medical attention.
If you forget to take Chloraseptic lozenges If you miss a dose of Chloraseptic lozenges, take it as soon as you remember. If it is almost time for your next dose, do not take the missed dose. Take the next dose at your regular time. Do not take two doses to make up for a missed dose.
Do not change the prescribed dose by yourself unless your doctor tells you to.
If you have any further questions on the use of this medicine, ask your doctor or pharmacist.


Like all medicines, Chloraseptic lozenges can cause side effects, although not everybody gets them.
Allergic reactions have been reported very occasionally with benzocaine.
There have been occasional reports of temporary breathing difficulty, and swelling of the face or mouth.
Methaemoglobinaemia has been rarely reported with benzocaine use.
If any of the side effects gets serious, or if you notice any side effects not listed in this leaflet, Please tell your doctor or pharmacist.


- Keep out of reach and sight of children.
- Store below 30°C.
- Do not use Chloraseptic lozenges after the expiry date which is stated on the carton .The Expiry date refers to the last day of that month
- Do not throw away any medicines via wastewater or household waste. Ask your pharmacist how to dispose of medicines you no longer use. These measures will help protect the environment.


The active substance in Chloraseptic lozenges are Benzocaine 6 mg & Menthol 10 mg

ATC code: R02AD01
Benzocaine is a local anaesthetic of the ester type. The mode of action is a
reversible inhibition of the flux of sodium and potassium ions through the
axonal membranes of sensory nerves from peripheral pain receptors. As a
consequence, the depolarisation and propagation of nerve impulses are
inhibited.
The onset of action of benzocaine on mucous membranes is rapid due to the
anaesthetic direct to the site of application with rapid absorption and the
surface analgesic effect. The local anaesthetic activity produced by benzocaine
acting on mucosal membranes is rapid, occurring within one minute and lasts
for 30 – 60 minutes. Chloraseptic Sore Throat Lozenges duration of action
depends on the manipulation and sucking action in the buccal cavity.
Menthol is a covalent organic compound made synthetically or obtained from
peppermint or other mint oils. Menthol's ability to chemically trigger coldsensitive
receptors in the skin is responsible for the well-known cooling
sensation that it provokes when inhaled, eaten, or applied to the skin. It should
be noted that menthol does not cause an actual drop in temperature.


Benzocaine is absorbed into the mucosal membranes. After systemic
absorption, which is negligible, the drug is rapidly metabolised to
p-aminobenzoic acid by plasma esterases. Aminobenzoic acid is excreted
unchanged or conjugated with glycine to aminohippuric acid in the liver, the
metabolites and unchanged benzocaine are excreted in the urine.


No animal data are available on benzocaine tthroat lozenges. Non-clinical
studies on benzocaine showed local irritation, sensation, and
methaemoglobinaemia at high doses in some species.
